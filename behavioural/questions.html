🪀 Common Questions

Full Q&A reference bank, ordered the way interviews actually flow: positioning → motivation → how I work → stories → leadership → self-awareness → AI → closing. For the five-minute scan before an interview, use Interview Flow. Delivery guidance lives in How to Prepare.

1 · Introduction & positioning

The intro — "tell me about yourself" (~1 minute)

Say this — short sentences, one breath each

"I'm a product-minded technical lead with around 16 years of experience. My core stack is React and TypeScript. I know enough backend and architecture to own a delivery end to end.

Right now I'm at Lantern, a B2B finance data platform. I take work from PRD to production. I help shape the specs. I design the frontend and integration boundaries. And I work with backend engineers on the APIs and validation.

I work spec-driven, with AI-assisted implementation under my review.

Now I'm looking for a role where engineers own product outcomes, move fast in ambiguity, and use AI seriously."

Memorise the beats, not the words

  • Whoproduct-minded technical lead, ~16 years — React/TS surface → enough backend/architecture to own full delivery
  • NowLantern, B2B finance data — PRD → production: shape specs · FE + integration boundaries · partner with BE on APIs & validation
  • Craftspec-driven + AI under my review — one clause, no more
  • Wantown · move · use — own outcomes · move fast in ambiguity · use AI seriously

WHO → NOW → CRAFT → WANT  ·  stop cleanly — let them pick the follow-up

Held back on purpose: the proof point — "first production release of calculated metrics in one sprint, after scope and foundations were agreed" — comes out when they ask for a recent example. If asked "why us?", answer with the three-reason pattern. More time / senior panel → add one line on mentoring after NOW. See How to Prepare for tone and pausing guidance.

AI gets one clause here ("with AI-assisted implementation under my review") — no more. Expand only if they ask; the detail lives in AI-assisted engineering. Leading with AI makes you an "AI workflow candidate" instead of a senior product engineer.

Positioning — full-stack, frontend-leaning

"I'm full-stack end to end. My delivery strength is the user-facing surface and the BFF contract — React, TypeScript, React Query. I design the API and data boundaries with backend engineers. I don't just hand off to them. I own the integration and the server-state strategy: query keys, invalidation, and consistency across product surfaces. I also own the auth-shaped responses. On calculated metrics I led the export and visualisation layer, and the backend service contracts."
  • Don't say: "I'm a frontend engineer" or "I'm backend-only"
  • Do say: full-stack owner · strong on the consumption layer + API contracts · comfortable in Python services

"What are you looking for — and not looking for — in your next role?"

Looking for: a product-focused engineering role where I stay hands-on. I want to solve meaningful problems, influence architecture, and work closely with Product, Design and Engineering. I want an environment where engineers are trusted to think beyond implementation and own outcomes. And a team that adopts AI-assisted engineering thoughtfully — AI improves delivery speed, but quality and judgement stay human-owned.

Not looking for: a role where engineering is handed tickets with little context or ownership. I do my best work when I understand the problem. I want to influence the solution. And I want to help the team improve how it builds software.

If hybrid/office expectations come up

"I'm comfortable with hybrid working. The office time should be purposeful: collaboration, planning, design discussions and team alignment."

"Why are you looking to move?" / "Why are you leaving?" — canonical answer (30 sec, positive framing)

"I've learned a lot at Lantern and I'm proud of what we built. I led a major platform delivery. I mentored engineers. I established spec-driven and AI-assisted practices the squad still uses. Now I'm looking for deeper product and engineering challenges, broader ownership, and continued hands-on growth. I'm not running from anything. I'm running toward higher ownership and impact."
  • Natural breakpoint: just shipped a major platform piece end-to-end — right moment for a new challenge
  • Right seniority: ready for senior IC ownership across a product domain, not single features
  • Growth, not escape: scale / consumer impact / AI-critical product — pick the axis that fits the company

Memorise the beats, not the words

  • Grown3 verbs — led a platform delivery · mentored engineers · established spec-driven + AI practices the squad still uses
  • Ready2 wants — deeper product + engineering challenges · broader ownership + hands-on growth
  • Frame1 flip — not running from anything → running toward higher ownership + impact (keep word-perfect)

GROWN (led · mentored · established) → READY (challenges · ownership) → TOWARD  ·  3 verbs, 2 wants, 1 flip

Never bad-mouth the current employer. Frame as: "I've grown here; ready for the next scale." If they probe notice period or timing, answer factually and keep the energy forward-looking.

2 · Motivation & company fit

"Why this company?" — the three-reason pattern (30 seconds, always the same shape)

Template "Three reasons. First, the problem — <why their domain matters and why it's underserved / hard / high-trust>. Second, the fit — <stack, product shape, or engineering culture that matches how I already work>. Third, the role — <the specific team/scope where my strengths (correctness, observability, customer-facing flows, AI-assisted delivery) create impact>."

The reasoning pattern is always: mission fit → product interest → stack/role fit. Fill each slot from research the night before; deliver conversationally.

I'm aligned on impact, not title inflation."

I've led delivery and want a tighter loop between engineering decision and customer outcome. Startups reward end-to-end ownership and spec-driven speed — how I already work. Add a mission I care about and a stack close enough to be productive immediately.

3 · How I work with Product & Engineering

How do you work with product managers?

Co-author the PRD. I bring technical constraints and acceptance criteria. The PM brings user and business context. I translate ambiguity into engineering-safe definitions. I push back with alternatives, not nos: "not 10 metrics in sprint one, but 3 with golden fixtures and a path to configurable definitions in iteration two."

If they follow with "give us an example" → Financial Reporting Discovery. Principle first, story second.

How do you ensure quality while keeping velocity high?

Build quality into the workflow, not into a final gate. That means small PRs, clear acceptance criteria, good automated checks, and early conversations about edge cases. Reusable patterns — API calls, error handling, testing, feature flags — mean engineers aren't reinventing decisions. AI helps on tests, mocks and boilerplate. Human review stays on architecture, security, and production behaviour.

The balance is not speed versus quality — good engineering practices usually improve both over time.

"How do you manage multiple priorities?"

Rank by, in order:

  1. Customer / business impact
  2. Urgency and severity — is anything degrading right now?
  3. Regulatory / security risk
  4. Dependencies — what unblocks other people
  5. Reversibility — irreversible decisions get time; reversible ones get speed
  6. Effort — last, as a tiebreaker
"Prioritisation should be visible and shared — not privately decided inside Engineering. I'd rather have a stakeholder disagree with the ranking than not know it exists."

Use for

Prioritisation · transparency · working with Product/TPM

"Tell me about a decision made with incomplete information"

"I separate the irreversible part from the reversible parts. Reversible decisions I make quickly and cheaply. For the irreversible one, I validate the riskiest assumption first. I document what we assumed. And I set a review point, so we course-correct on evidence rather than opinion."

Full story: Handling Ambiguity.

Use for

Ambiguity · startup pace · judgement under uncertainty

"How do you say no?" (Product/TPM interviews)

"I avoid a bare no. I explain the constraint and the risk the request creates. Then I offer an alternative that still supports the outcome they're after. Usually that's a smaller slice now, with a path to the rest."

Worked example: Scope Under Pressure — "not 10 metrics in sprint one, but 3 with golden fixtures and a path to configurable definitions."

Use for

Stakeholder management · scope negotiation · collaboration with Product

How do you measure product impact? (feature-level)

Quantitative: product analytics (usage/export events), support-ticket tags, time studies on the workflow you replaced. Qualitative: customers using in-product numbers in their own external materials. Technical: no production incidents, latency improvements, patterns adopted by other squads. Instrument success metrics in the PRD on day one.

How much should people be allowed to make mistakes?

Mistakes are fine where impact is controlled and learning is real. That means local dev, test environments, small reversible changes, internal tooling. Customer data, payments, security and production reliability need stronger guardrails. Good culture creates safe boundaries: review, testing, feature flags, monitoring, rollback plans, blameless learning. When something goes wrong, I care about two things. Did we understand why? And did we improve the system?

"The goal is not zero mistakes. The goal is controlled risk and fast learning."

What distinguishes senior engineers?

Ownership beyond their PR — product outcomes, unblocking others, making good scope calls, leaving the codebase better. (Mirror this in how you answer — it's likely what they're listening for.)

"What problem did you solve that had the biggest combination of technical, organisational and product complexity?"

Hook — open with this "The hardest problem I've solved wasn't a single technical bug. It was transforming an existing product into something an engineering organisation could actually scale."

The situation

  • Joined Lantern with the product already live — large parts built by external agencies
  • It worked, but every new feature increased regression risk
  • Everything tightly coupled → teams couldn't work independently → delivery getting slower, not faster

The goal — not a rewrite

Create an engineering platform where multiple teams could build features safely and independently — while the business kept delivering to customers.

What I did — people first, then architecture

  1. Understand before proposing — spent time on the technical architecture and on how teams actually worked. Aligned with Product, backend engineers and engineering leadership before any technical proposal
  2. Micro-frontend architecture — independently deployable services
  3. Clear contracts between frontend and backend
  4. Shared design system + common engineering standards
  5. Incremental migration — no risky big-bang rewrite
  6. AI-assisted development last — only after the foundations. PRD-driven workflow: Product + Engineering jointly refine requirements → AI generates implementation → every change through human technical and product review

If challenged — "why micro-frontends? Don't they add complexity?"

"We chose independently deployable frontend domains because teams had distinct ownership boundaries and release needs. The organisational shape justified the architectural cost. I wouldn't use micro-frontends for a single team or a tightly coupled product."

The result

  • Engineering grew from essentially one frontend engineer → ~20 engineers across four product teams
  • Teams deliver independently; frontend prototypes validated before backend implementation
  • Throughput up significantly without losing ownership of quality
Why it was the hardest — close with this "The technical solution was only one part. The bigger challenge was aligning people and changing how teams worked — while the product kept evolving. It required technical judgement, communication and patience much more than writing code."

HOOK → coupled legacy → platform goal → people first → 5 moves (MFE · contracts · design system · incremental · AI last) → 1 → 20 engineers → CLOSE

Follow-up — "What made it difficult?"

  • No pause button — couldn't stop feature delivery; customers were already on the platform
  • Alignment — several teams had to agree common standards instead of everyone solving problems differently
  • Stability — improve the architecture incrementally without destabilising existing customers

Full STAR version: Platform Rebuild story.

5 · Leadership & stakeholders

"Tell me about a disagreement with Product"

Shape: Product wanted the full scope on the original date. I disagreed on feasibility. Instead of a flat no, I laid out the customer impact of each option, the risk profile, and a phased alternative that protected the launch date. We shipped the phase-one slice on time, and the rest on evidence.

"Disagreement with Product isn't a turf question — it's a data question. I bring the customer impact, the risk, and an alternative. Once we decide, I commit fully."

Stories: Financial Reporting (disagreement on what the problem was) · Scope Under Pressure (disagreement on scope vs date).

6 · Mistakes, feedback & self-awareness

"What was one of the biggest mistakes you've made in your career?" — primary answer

Hook — open with this "One of the biggest mistakes I made earlier in my career was jumping into implementation too quickly because I thought I already understood the problem."

What happened

  • A stakeholder explained a feature → I immediately started on the technical solution
  • Built a large part of it — and technically it worked well
  • At the user demo: we'd solved the requirement they described, not the problem they were trying to solve
  • Had to change a significant part of the implementation — we'd never validated our assumptions about the user's workflow

What changed since

  • Business outcome before architecture — much more time understanding the problem first
  • Talk directly to Product / customers where possible: who is the user? what does success look like? what are we assuming?
  • Shaped how I introduced AI-assisted development — AI doesn't write code until the PRD has been refined several times by both Product and Engineering
Lesson — close with this "Engineers shouldn't optimise for writing code first. We should optimise for understanding the problem first. A well-understood problem is far more valuable than a quickly generated solution."

HOOK → jumped to solution → worked technically, wrong problem → rework → outcome-first + validate assumptions → PRD before AI → LESSON

⚠ Fill before use — ground it in a real project

  • What the feature was
  • Who the user was
  • What was misunderstood
  • What had to be rebuilt
  • What it cost — time, trust, delivery

Right now this is a pattern, not a grounded event. Interviewers will probe for specifics — prepare them in advance, never invent them in the room.

Alternative answer — the over-engineering mistake (more technical)

Use when the interviewer is technical or the "wrong problem" story has already come up.

Hook "Earlier in my career I believed good architecture meant building something that could solve every future problem."
  • Designed systems that were technically elegant but more flexible than the business needed
  • Looking back: spent time solving problems that didn't exist yet
  • Realised over time: simple architecture with clear extension points beats predicting every future requirement
Lesson "Today I build the smallest solution that solves today's problem well, while leaving room to evolve when we have real evidence."

"Tell me about a time you received difficult feedback"

"In a recent interview process I got direct feedback. My technical thinking was strong, but my answers took too long to reach the point. I changed my approach: answer first, pause, then add context. Later interview feedback specifically praised my communication."
  • Why this works: recent, specific, and shows measurable behavioural change — not a generic "I welcome feedback"
  • Alternative: early-career feedback that I moved too quickly into solutions → ties into the biggest-mistake arc

Use for

Coachability · self-awareness · communication growth

What are your weaknesses?

Earlier in my career I moved too quickly into implementation, before fully understanding the problem. Then I over-corrected for a while. I spent too long exploring options, because I wanted to understand every trade-off. Today I time-box discovery. I validate the riskiest assumptions first. I decide once the remaining uncertainty is acceptable.

This deliberately connects to the biggest mistake story — one arc (rushed → over-corrected → calibrated), not two contradictory flaws. If both come up in one interview, name the arc explicitly.

"Tell me about a failure" (distinct from "biggest mistake")

A mistake is a bad decision; a failure is an outcome that fell short. Shape to use:

  • Feature technically completed and shipped — but adoption was weak because user discovery was insufficient
  • We measured the low usage honestly instead of declaring victory
  • Simplified the flow based on what users actually did, and adoption improved
"Shipping isn't the outcome — adoption is. We treated the launch as a hypothesis, measured it, and fixed it."

Fill this with a real project you can defend in every detail before using it — do not improvise specifics in the room.

Use for

Failure · measuring impact · intellectual honesty

"Tell me about a production incident" (highly likely for Lead roles)

Structure to walk through — have one real incident prepared against it:

  1. Customer-visible issue — what users saw, how it surfaced
  2. Severity assessment — blast radius, data risk, who's affected
  3. Communication & ownership — who I informed, taking point
  4. Mitigation first — restore service before root-causing
  5. Root-cause analysis — blameless, evidence preserved
  6. Prevention — the guardrail, test, or alert that now exists
"During an incident I separate restoration from diagnosis: restore service first, then investigate fully without losing evidence."

Use for

On-call ownership · calm under pressure · blameless culture

"What would your manager say about you?" (asked at zeroheight)

"They'd probably say three things. I bring structure to ambiguous problems. I take ownership beyond my immediate tasks. And I help other engineers move faster. They'd also say I sometimes challenge assumptions strongly. But I do it because I care about the outcome. And I support the final decision once it's made."

Strength + honest edge + disagree-and-commit close. Keep the edge genuine — it pre-empts the reference check.

Use for

Self-awareness · references alignment · team fit

What do you admire in peers? / What should peers improve?

Admire: engineers who combine curiosity with ownership — technically strong, ask good questions, care about the customer outcome, take responsibility beyond their own ticket, and challenge ideas constructively.

Improve: surfacing assumptions and trade-offs earlier. Engineers sometimes jump into implementation before the team agrees what problem is being solved. I encourage lightweight design notes and ADRs — not heavy process, just enough alignment to avoid rework.

"I'd like teams to make implicit assumptions explicit earlier."

Frame the improvement as constructive, not critical.

How do you currently use AI? / What role does AI play day-to-day?

  • Cursor — primary IDE: inline refactors, multi-file changes, test generation from SDD context
  • Claude Code — agentic tasks: scaffold service modules, explore unfamiliar code paths, batch test fixes
  • AIAD workflow — PRD/SDD checked into the repo → AI implements to spec → human review → CI gates merge
  • Also: documentation drafts, refactor option exploration, OpenAPI client boilerplate — AI explores, I choose and merge

Guardrails: no customer/PII data in prompts · full CI before merge · every AI diff reviewed · architecture and business rules never AI-generated without explicit review · I own merge, deploy, and production behaviour.

"AI helps me move faster, but I still own the outcome. I review AI output the way I'd review a junior engineer's code: useful, but not automatically trusted."

How would you rebuild a previous project using AI? — the concrete example

Walk the pipeline on the calculated-metrics story: (1) PRD with PM — problem, users, acceptance criteria, success metrics. (2) SDD — architecture, API contracts, validation rules, 4-layer test plan. (3) AI-assisted implementation — scaffolding, mocks, BFF routes, test parity from SDD context. (4) Review — every diff; metric definitions and calculation logic human-written. (5) Testing — 50+ tests, CI on every commit, golden fixtures from customer models. (6) Monitoring — error tracking and dashboards from day one; on-call through launch.

"AI compressed implementation time. It did not replace the spec, the architecture, or the production sign-off. The first production release landed in one sprint. That was possible because scope and foundations were agreed before AI touched code."

What humans own vs what AI accelerates

AI accelerates

  • Boilerplate & scaffolding
  • Test & mock generation
  • Refactor exploration
  • Documentation drafts
  • Repetitive adapter code

Humans own

  • Architecture & system boundaries
  • Production decisions & rollouts
  • Quality — correctness, security, compliance
  • Business rules & validation logic
  • Merge, deploy, on-call, incidents

What are your AI guardrails?

  1. Spec before codegen
  2. No PII / customer data in prompts
  3. Full CI before merge — lint, types, tests
  4. Human review on every AI diff
  5. Business rules and compliance logic authored or explicitly reviewed by an engineer
  6. I deploy and am on-call — incentive alignment for quality

"How would you measure whether an AI tool / internal tool improves productivity?" (memorise list)

Define metrics before shipping; baseline first:

  • Time saved per workflow
  • Developer adoption (DAU/WAU, retention after first use)
  • Pull-request cycle time · lead time for changes (DORA-style flow metrics)
  • Reduction in repeated manual tasks
  • Error reduction · quality signals — defects, rework, CI failures on AI-assisted code
  • Satisfaction feedback — especially from skeptics
"I'd baseline before launch. I'd pick two quantitative metrics and one qualitative metric per tool. Then review at 4 weeks — kill or double down based on adoption, not demo applause."

If you quote a throughput number ("~2 features per sprint → ~10") — always attach the caveat

"It wasn't a controlled experiment, and the features varied in size. So I don't treat it as a precise productivity multiplier. What I can say: throughput clearly improved during comparable delivery periods. Cycle time got shorter, and we completed more product slices."

Never state the multiplier without the caveat — an unqualified 5× claim reads as inflated and invites a credibility probe. Caveat first or immediately after, then move on.

What do you do with time saved through AI?

  • Architecture — whiteboard trade-offs, ADRs, cross-team boundaries before code
  • Product discussions — sharper PRDs, clearer acceptance criteria with PM
  • Validation — golden fixtures, edge-case review, correctness over velocity
  • Stakeholder engagement — scope docs, design partners, alignment on MVP
  • Quality — more test coverage, observability, documentation — not more tickets closed
"AI buys implementation speed. I reinvest it in judgement: architecture, product clarity, and quality. That's what keeps production safe at pace."

Anti-pattern to avoid: "I take on more stories." Good answer: higher-leverage work — e.g. tests shipped in the same merge instead of a follow-up PR.

What is the role of software engineers in an AI world?

  • Less typing code — more specifying intent and reviewing output
  • More judgement — what to build, what not to build, what ships
  • More systems thinking — boundaries, failure modes, data flows
  • More validation — tests, fixtures, observability — AI output is plausible but not trustworthy by default
  • More product understanding — engineers closer to "why", not just "how"
"The engineer becomes editor-in-chief of the system. AI drafts. Humans own correctness, architecture, and customer trust. In regulated, high-trust domains this matters more, not less."

Reference — definitions worth using consistently

  • Specification-driven development — lock problem, acceptance criteria, and contracts before implementation; code follows spec, not the reverse
  • PRD-driven development — the product requirements doc defines users, success metrics, scope and definition-of-done; engineering extends it with technical constraints
  • AIAD (AI-Assisted Development) — AI executes against written specs under engineer review; CI and human sign-off gate production

Reference — how much of your code is AI-generated?

AI accelerated repetitive work: scaffolding, mocks, tests, adapter boilerplate. It saved roughly half the time on those tasks. Architecture, contracts, validation rules, security boundaries and production config stayed engineer-owned. I don't quote a percentage of lines. That's the wrong metric. The right metric: did we ship with full test parity and no production incidents? Yes.

Reference — how would AI change engineering at a new company?

I'd expect AI on boilerplate: handlers, test fixtures, scaffolding. Engineers own domain correctness, customer journey design, and observability. I'd advocate for shared spec-driven practices and measured adoption. Don't mandate tools. Document what works, so the team compounds velocity safely.

8 · Future contribution & closing

What would you do in your first 30 days?

Listen — codebase, team workflows, on-call patterns, how AI is used today. Ship a small meaningful improvement with review from the squad. Document one workflow improvement (spec template, test pattern, observability gap). Build trust before proposing larger architectural changes.

I still see myself as a technologist. I enjoy staying close to technology and solving problems. I expect I'll have broader influence and responsibility than I do today. But I want to stay involved in architecture, engineering and product decisions, rather than moving away from technology.

"You need someone who can own the frontend and full-stack direction. Someone who works closely with Product, and helps several teams deliver consistently. My background combines exactly those three things: hands-on technical depth, product thinking, and experience creating standards that help other engineers move faster."

This question usually comes near the end — treat it as a summary of everything already discussed. Reference back to specifics from the conversation ("as we talked about with the platform work…") rather than introducing new claims.

9 · Industry-specific answers

"Why financial products?" — domain motivation (for finance-domain companies)

"Finance products have the hardest correctness bar. A wrong number isn't a UX bug — it's a trust incident. That forces better engineering: validation at the boundary, auditable data flows, and server-side truth. Server-side truth means every surface uses the same calculation logic. Nobody reimplements formulas in dashboards, exports or spreadsheets. I find that constraint motivating. The domain makes you a better engineer."
  • High stakes = better craft: correctness, auditability, explicit trade-offs matter more than in consumer apps
  • Real users, real workflows: professionals depend on the product for decisions — not vanity metrics
  • Data + rules complexity: transforms, aggregations, FX, roll-ups — interesting engineering, not CRUD
  • Already in the domain: B2B finance platform — calculated metrics, reporting, portfolio analytics

Company-specific — only for finance-domain interviews. For other domains, adapt the same shape: hardest constraint of their domain → why that constraint makes engineering better → your track record with it.

"Why this industry?" — when the domain has a bad reputation (acknowledge → reframe → purpose)

Pattern for any domain people side-eye (debt collection, gambling-adjacent, ad-tech…):

  1. Acknowledge the reputation honestly — the default playbook in the industry is genuinely bad
  2. Reframe — this company's mission is to fix the industry with technology, transparency and better customer journeys. Not to do the bad thing more efficiently
  3. Purpose — connect to your track record of building trust in products (validated numbers, visible failure modes). Say you want that skill applied where it changes real people's experience
"You're right — the industry's default playbook is aggressive and opaque. That's exactly why I'm interested. The mission isn't to do the same thing harder. It's to give people clarity, dignity, and workable paths forward. I've spent my career building trust in financial products. I want that same discipline applied to outcomes that change someone's day-to-day life."

This question tests values, not domain expertise. Be sincere — no stigma tourism, no bad-mouthing incumbents generically.

10 · Questions to ask the interviewer

How to choose — 2–3 questions, matched to the audience

  • Reference the conversation — "you mentioned X earlier…" beats any prepared question
  • Never ask anything answerable from the website, and don't ask about compensation/process here
  • Signal your positioning — every question below reinforces product-minded ownership

Starter set — pick by who's in the room

For engineers / peers

  • "What does the path from idea to production look like here — who writes the spec, who reviews, who deploys?"
  • "Where does quality actually get enforced — review culture, CI, or ownership norms?"
  • "How is AI used in the team today — and what's worked or flopped?"

For the hiring manager

  • "What would a great first six months look like — what would you want shipped or changed?"
  • "What's the hardest thing about this team's roadmap right now?"
  • "How do engineers influence what gets built, not just how?"

For Product / TPM

  • "How do Product and Engineering resolve scope disagreements here?"
  • "What's a recent example of the team cutting scope well — or badly?"

  • <question about a recent launch, announcement or engineering blog post>
