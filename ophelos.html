Deep-Dive · AI Questions

How would you rebuild a previous project using AI?
Lantern calculated metrics: (1) PRD with PM — problem, users, acceptance criteria, success metrics. (2) SDD — server-side calc architecture, OpenAPI contracts, validation-at-boundary, 4-layer test plan. (3) AI implementation — Cursor for MSW handlers, BFF routes, hook/component test scaffolding from SDD. (4) Review — I reviewed every diff; metric definitions and calculation logic human-written. (5) Testing — 50+ tests, CI on every commit, golden fixtures from customer models. (6) Monitoring — Sentry + Grafana, on-call through design-partner phase, zero P1 at GA. Outcome: one sprint discovery-to-production because spec was locked before AI touched code.
What do you do with time saved through AI?
Reinvest in higher-leverage work: architecture whiteboarding and ADRs before coding; sharper PRD/scope conversations with PM; golden fixtures and edge-case validation; stakeholder alignment docs; observability and test parity in the same PR instead of a follow-up. Anti-pattern: using saved time to cram more low-quality tickets. At Lantern, AI time savings meant every contract change shipped with 4-layer test parity in one merge — we didn't defer quality.
What is the role of software engineers in an AI world?
Engineers shift from primary authors to editors and architects of systems. Less keystroke volume; more judgement on what to build, how components interact, and what "correct" means. More validation — tests, fixtures, monitoring — because AI output is plausible but not trustworthy by default. More product proximity — engineers who understand customer outcomes make better spec and review decisions. In regulated or high-trust domains (payments, debt resolution), this matters more, not less.
How do you currently use AI?
Cursor as primary IDE for inline edits, multi-file refactors, and test generation from SDD context. Claude Code for agentic scaffolding — new service modules, exploring unfamiliar code paths, batch test fixes. AIAD workflow: PRD/SDD checked into repo → AI implements to spec → I review → CI gates merge. Also: documentation drafts, refactor option exploration, OpenAPI client boilerplate. Guardrails: no customer/fund data in prompts; full CI; I own merge and production behaviour; architecture and business rules never AI-generated without review.
How much of your code is AI-generated?
AI accelerated repetitive work — scaffolding, mocks, tests, adapter boilerplate — roughly half the time on those tasks. Architecture, contracts, validation rules, security boundaries, and production config stayed engineer-owned. I don't quote a percentage of lines — that's the wrong metric. The right metric is: did we ship with full test parity and zero P1? Yes.
What are your AI guardrails?
(1) Spec before codegen. (2) No PII/customer data in prompts. (3) Full CI before merge — lint, types, tests. (4) Human review on every AI diff. (5) Business rules and compliance logic authored or explicitly reviewed by engineer. (6) I deploy and am on-call — incentive alignment for quality.
How would AI change engineering at Ophelos?
I'd expect AI on boilerplate — API handlers, test fixtures, workflow scaffolding — while engineers own payment correctness, customer journey design, Temporal workflow semantics, and observability. I'd advocate for shared spec-driven practices and measured adoption — not mandating tools, but documenting what works in Payments so the squad compounds velocity safely.

Deep-Dive · Startup Questions

Tell me about shipping quickly with limited information.
Calculated metrics: requirements were broad ("configurable KPIs for all customers"). I narrowed to 3 investor-ready KPIs with golden fixtures as definition-of-done, ran design partners under a feature flag mid-sprint, and GA'd at sprint end. Limited info didn't mean guessing — it meant writing the smallest testable scope, getting sign-off, and learning from real usage.
Tell me about a trade-off you made.
Two-tier read model: pinned metrics use materialised snapshots (~120ms p95); ad-hoc metrics calculate live (3–8s). Trade-off: brief staleness on hot path vs sub-second dashboard UX. We show lastCalculatedAt so users trust the freshness contract. Alternative rejected: client-side calc — killed after spike showed FX month-end parity failure.
How do you operate in ambiguity?
Write a scope doc with testable acceptance criteria. Facilitate alignment between PM (breadth) and stakeholders who need auditability. Time-box spikes for unknowns — 2h client-side calc spike, half-day warehouse spike I'd run earlier next time. Ship to design partners, not to all tenants, when correctness risk is high.
How do you learn from customers?
Design partners before full rollout — surfaced FX and roll-up edge cases. Golden fixtures built from real customer Excel models, not synthetic data. Post-launch: export event analytics, CS ticket tags, time studies on reporting workflows. Qualitative signal: customers quoting in-product IRR in board materials.
Why startup vs larger company?
I've led delivery at Lantern and want tighter loop between engineering decision and customer outcome. Startups reward end-to-end ownership and spec-driven speed — how I already work. Ophelos adds a mission I care about and a stack close enough to be productive immediately.
What would you do in your first 30 days?
Listen — codebase, Payments flows, on-call patterns, how AI is used today. Ship a small meaningful improvement with review from the squad. Document one workflow improvement (spec template, test pattern, observability gap). Build trust before proposing larger architectural changes.

Deep-Dive · Product Questions

Tell me about a feature you owned end-to-end.
Calculated metrics platform at Lantern. Situation: customers didn't trust in-product KPIs, rebuilt in Excel. Task: deliver configurable, server-validated metrics with export. Action: authored PRD/SDD with PM, designed definition schema + calc engine + validation layer + BFF + export UI, AI-assisted implementation under review, deployed to K8s, on-call through launch. Result: ~65% adoption in 5 weeks, reporting time 4–6h→~20min, ~30% ticket drop, zero P1 at GA, patterns reused by another squad.
How do you deal with ambiguity?
Convert ambiguity into written, testable scope. Example: "10 metric types sprint one" vs "investor-ready numbers" — I wrote a scope doc defining MVP as 3 KPIs (IRR, MOIC, net return) with golden fixtures and validation metadata as DoD. Finance signed off because success was testable, not subjective.
How do you prioritise?
Customer trust and correctness first, then adoption breadth. Instrument success metrics in PRD on day one (lesson learned — we backfilled some analytics post-launch). Explicit "not now" list — killed client-side calc, chart builder, self-serve authoring UI from sprint one to protect DoD.
How do you decide what to build?
Start from customer workaround — Excel models meant measurable pain. Quantify via CS tickets and time studies. Validate assumptions with PM and domain stakeholders. Prototype only after spec spike when technical risk is unknown. Design partners before GA when trust is on the line.
How do you measure impact?
Quantitative: product analytics (export events ~65% adoption), CS ticket tags (~30% drop month one), time study (8 accounts, quarterly pack 4–6h→~20min). Qualitative: customers using in-product numbers in external materials. Technical: zero P1, p95 latency improvement on dashboards, patterns adopted by another squad.
How do you work with product managers?
Co-author PRD — I bring technical constraints and acceptance criteria; PM brings user and business context. I translate ambiguity into engineering-safe definitions. Push back with alternatives, not nos — "not 10 metrics in sprint one, but 3 with golden fixtures and a path to configurable defs in iteration 2."
